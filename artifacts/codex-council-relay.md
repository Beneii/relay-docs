RESULT: Beautiful execution but I'd hold off betting because maintainability gaps and product risk outweigh the polish.
CODE QUALITY: The repo shows care: shared product limits feed both front end and backend, the dashboard screen delegates data fetching to hooks so the JSX in src/pages/Dashboard.tsx:1 stays mostly presentational, and the billing/email utilities in api/_billing.ts:1 and api/_email.ts:1 are written in a testable, dependency-injected style. However, the marketing surface is a 500+ line monolith (src/App.tsx:1) that mixes data, motion choreography, and layout with no compositional boundaries, which makes copy updates and A/B experiments risky. Pricing logic (src/pages/Pricing.tsx:1) duplicates feature descriptions alongside FREE_LIMITS/PRO_LIMITS and performs Supabase/Stripe orchestration directly in the component, so business changes require edits in several places and there is no unit coverage guarding payment failures. The CSS tokens in src/index.css:1 are solid, but I couldn't find automated tests or stories for the landing components, meaning regressions would only be caught manually. Overall, the codebase feels crafted but leans on hero components and manual QA.
ARCHITECTURE: The surface split (Vite marketing + dashboard, Expo mobile app, Supabase backend, Vercel API) is coherent and keeps the serverless pieces small; billing flows reuse a single handler factory and Stripe webhook processing is idempotent and defensive (api/_stripeWebhook.ts:1). Still, there are structural tensions: the marketing bundle owns onboarding plus authenticated flows, so every deploy ships everything and there is no clear boundary between marketing copy and product shell; and relying on Supabase service-role keys from edge functions for every paid action becomes a central point of failure without rate limiting or abuse controls. There's no background worker or queue for notifications or plan enforcement—everything hinges on Supabase triggers/functions, so any downtime there strands the entire experience. Nothing is egregiously wrong, but the architecture is still the “starter kit” version rather than a hardened system.
PRODUCT IDEA: Turning arbitrary dashboards into “native-feeling” apps with webhook-initiated push sounds attractive to power users, yet the addressable market is narrow. Grafana, Home Assistant, and most CI/CD tools already ship native or responsive experiences, and anyone technical enough to set up Relay can likely configure native push from those ecosystems or wrap sites in a PWA/container themselves. Relay’s differentiator—linking push notifications to saved webviews—depends on third-party dashboards being mobile-friendly (hence the best-practice copy in src/App.tsx:96), but that's outside Relay’s control, and the onboarding story still requires Tailscale/VPN plus custom webhooks per dashboard. Monetizing at $7.99/mo competes with “I’ll just bookmark it” behavior and with existing notification bridges (IFTTT, Pushover, ntfy, etc.) that already have deep integrations and trust.
OVERALL: I like the polish and the technical bar, yet I wouldn’t invest until the team shows either a drastically clearer wedge (e.g., pre-built templates or first-party monitoring) or evidence of retention beyond early adopters; right now it feels like an elegant hammer searching for nails.
